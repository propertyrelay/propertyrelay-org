Summary

"Distributed ledger technology has the potential to transform the delivery of public and private services."[7] Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT), of which Blockchain is a part, has been referred to as "...the Internet of value." [10] DLT is being analysed by many different organisations at home and abroad for use for many different applications involving value. The UK Government's Chief Scientist has issued a report about the technology. Sweden's Land Registry is investigating: 'Sweden conducts trials of a blockchain smart contracts technology for land registry' [4] and 'Estonia has an electronic land registry...reducing land transfers from three months to a little over a week.'[5]

These organisations are looking at different forms of DLT. Both private and public DLTs are being investigated, however there is a move to use Open Source Software(OSS) that is the basis for public DLTs. OSS is open to anyone, who can make contributions and use it freely. The United States government is making guidelines for its agencies regarding OSS. A memo was released on 8th August 2016 by Tony Scott, Whitehouse CIO, about open sourcing up to 20% of government agency's software[6]. Blockchain, is the basis of the two leading OSS projects by Market Capitalisation, Bitcoin and Ethereum, but Ethereum also implements Smart Contracts.

Introduction

The Blockchain is an immutable distributed database encompassing associated technologies that have the potential to transform and greatly improve the real property transaction ecosystem. At its center is the storage and transfer of ownership, of which the Land Registry is a very important part. The blockchain-based technology that can significantly improve the transfer of ownership of real property is called a smart contract.

At the moment the real property transaction process - the sale of a property - typically is a long process, taking about 2 months on average. Improvements in the process have been made with advances in technology. For example the Land Registry offers the AP1 form and the use of electronic conveyancing and electronic signatures as introduced by the Land Registration Act 2002.

Blockchain technologies change the dynamic of the relationships between the involved parties of the real property transaction. It allows peer to peer transactions, with an altered role for the 3rd parties. What this means for the Land Registry is that key parts of the title to land and property would be stored in a smart contract on the blockchain in the form of a hash, with a transformed and improved way of transferring ownership.

This arrangement could reduce the real property transaction time down to days, rather than months. The Land Registry could charge a fixed fee or introduce a scaled charging structure based on the timeframe of the transaction. The quicker and/or more involved the required response and resolution the higher the charge. The fee structure could be fully transparent, and updatable depending on demand. Charges could be shown to the buyer so they have the choice. Blockchain-based technology also could help automate and speed up the collection of taxes.

Property has significant value, it is governed by laws, and its transfer can be complicated. This means that there are a number of parties involved in the transaction process. In each transaction usually there is the buyer and seller, the Land Registry, solicitors, banks, Estate Agents and a surveyor. The transaction process is varied based on the property and the circumstances of those involved in the process. But many real property transactions involve exceptions that have standardised, quick and/or formalised, responses. This lends the real property process to automation using Blockchain-based, and Information, Technology.

For most of its history land and property registration with the Land Registry has be voluntary. Now only when an unregistered property is sold is there a requirement to register the property. This same voluntary approach can be taken with Blockchain technology. Sellers can choose whether to use the Blockchain allowing a gradual transition. However, the benefits and convenience of using this new technology, combined with ubiqutous access to technologies including computers and mobile phones that are connected to the Internet, will hopefully pull sellers onto the platform. And once a property has been put on the blockchain, subsequent transactions involving that property should be a lot quicker and streamlined.

Land Registry

The purpose of the Land Registry is to maintain a register of ownership of land and property in England and Wales. The Land Registry has been through a lot of changes since it was formed in 1862. Inititally the registry was in the form of paper title deeds and plans. Subsequently records of titles were transferred onto a database, where over 24 million records are currently maintained.

There are two factors about the Land Registry that lends itself to the conversion to a Blockchain model. The Land Registry:

  • maintains a register of land and property ownership in a database;
  • changes the state of the register in that database, representing transfer of ownership.

These two factors, the storage of records in a database, and the transfer of ownership in this database, can be implemented using Blockchain.

Blockchain

A blockchain—originally, block chain—is a distributed database that maintains a continuously-growing list of data records secured from tampering and revision. Each block contains a timestamp and a link to a previous block.[1]

As it relates to the business of the Land Registry, the ownership details in land and property records can be stored and transferred on the blockchain. In addition to ownership information, records in the form of electronic cash transactions can be stored and transferred on the blockchain. With these two factors there is the possiblity of exchange between two parties, the buyer and seller of the property, entirely on the blockchain without many of the procedures presently carried out by third parties. In it's initial incarnation, introduced in 2008, blockchain referred to a growing database of electronic cash transactions called Bitcoin.

Bitcoin

"Bitcoin is a peer-to-peer electronic cash system."[9] "...and transactions take place between users directly, without an intermediary. These transactions are verified by network nodes and recorded in a public distributed ledger called the blockchain, which uses bitcoin as its unit of account. "[8]

Within the context of the Land Registry, Bitcoin can be seen as the payment, an electronic cash transaction, in the real property transaction. The advantages of using this mechanism is that it's secure, quick, incurs low fees, and is transparent.

Bitcoin transactions confirm on average in 10 minutes, but depends on the network. A confirmation is similar in nature to clearing in a bank. The greater the number of confirmations the greater the permanence of the transaction. As the payments for real property are significant value 6 confirmations or more is advisable. Transaction fees are very low at the moment. Well under a $1 per transaction. Transactions on the bitcoin blockchain are transparent, meaning they can be seen by anyone.

Because of the way Bitcoin and blockchains are designed it can interact with other DLTs. With the electonic cash payment recorded in the database, which is immutable and timestamped, this bitcoin payment information can be verified and used by the DLT that enables automation of the real property transaction is called a Smart Contract.

Smart Contracts

"Smart contracts are computer protocols that facilitate, verify, or enforce the negotiation or performance of a contract, or that make a contractual clause unnecessary. Smart contracts usually also have a user interface and often emulate the logic of contractual clauses." [3]

The DLT that automates the transfer of ownership of the property and storage of the owners details on the blockchain, the other side of the tranaction, is handled by Smart Contracts. This automated transfer of ownership is triggered by the fullfillment of the requirement specified by the owner in the Smart Contract. i.e. the payment of the sale price of the real property, in bitcoins.

The function of a Smart Contract is best explained with a comparison between a current process of selling an item, in Diagram 1, and how the process of a Smart Contract would be applied to it, in Diagram 2. Diagram 2 is a hypothectical process that contains a hypothectical entity called an Augmented Independent Repository (AIR) that executes functions based on the actions of the parties involved. Basically this repository ensures that if the seller recieves the payment the item is automatically sent to the buyer, whoever that is.

Analogy of operation of a Smart Contract - the Augmented Independent Repository (AIR)

Diagram 1: Current transaction process

Seller posts for-sale advert
 
Buyer sends payment to seller
 
Seller sends item to seller

Diagram 2: Hypothetical AIR process

Seller posts for-sale advert
Seller sends item to repository
Buyer sends payment to seller
Repository gets payment notification
Repository sends item to seller

The process of selling something with nominal value probably doesn't need the added processes introduced in Diagram 2, but exchanging things of considerable value, this escrow-type service would provide considerable security to both the seller and buyer. In particular, the AIR only releases the item when the seller recieves the payment. And the buyer is automatically sent the item when the seller recieves the payment.

Real property has considerable value, and becuase of the design of the blockchain, digital cash and smart contracts, the process similar to that shown in Diagram 2 can be translated to a real property transaction. Instead of transferring a physical item, the smart contract transfers the ownership of real property in exchange for payment. All done on the blockchain, reducing the process down to days. The OSS DLT that has the second highest market cap to Bitcoin, and implements Smart Contacts, is Ethereum.

Ethereum

"Ethereum is a public blockchain-based distributed computing platform, featuring smart contract functionality. It provides a decentralized virtual machine, the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM), that can execute peer-to-peer contracts using a cryptocurrency called ether."[11]

Ethereum implements smart contracts. In the context of the Land Registry, in exchange for a payment in bitcoin to the seller of the real property, the smart contract transfers ownership from the seller to the buyer, and stores this information on the blockchain.

Bitcoin and Ethereum have separate blockchains. So to a bridge is needed between them to verify the bitcoin payment on the Ethereum blockchain by relaying the transaction to the smart contract. This bridge is called BTC Relay.

The following diagram shows the real property transaction processs:

Arrow Flow Diagram of buyer/seller transaction process

Seller calls smart contract, advertising their real property and their bitcoin address
Buyer sends bitcoin to sellers bitcoin address
BTC Relay verifys buyers bitcoin transaction to sellers bitcoin address
Ethereum contract executes registering the buyers new ownership of the property
time taken: < 60 minutes

In the first step of the above diagram, the relevant smart contract is called by the seller. Before a contract can be called it must be written and deployed to the blockchain. The contract in this case would be written by the Land Registry. Once a contract is deployed on the blockchain it is permanent and immutable. But contracts can call other contracts. And also a varible in a contract can be coded that allows links to any contract.

In the second step the buyer sends bitcoin to the sellers bitcoin address, given by the seller. This is done by executing a bitcoin transaction. The transaction contains a field called OP_RETURN that can hold data. This data field would contain identifier details of the buyer. The buyers Ethereum address would be included and the address of the property and the buyers name. Other data could be added but because of the size contraints of the data field it may be necessary to store a hash of the data.

The third step involves relaying the confirmation of the bitcoin transaction to the above mentioned smart contract. Bitcoin has been used as the payment in this example, but if Ether, Ethereum's currency, was used in step 2 this process would be simpler. i.e. removes the need for BTC Relay.

The fourth step is where the contract executes putting the buyers details on the blockchain. Smart contracts are immutable but they also have a small amount of mutable storage. In fact a contract can hold 2^261 possible records of real properties in the form of hashes. The ownership of each real property would use one of these records. This data would be the proof of ownership of a real property.

These 4 steps are a greatly simplified representation of the real property transaction process. Many processes before and and during this process are needed. For example checks by the surveyor need to be performed before a property is sold. And also if a property chain is involved, this changes the dynamic of the process. There are also questions about whether properties are on blockchain or off. Also, in law property transactions have to be in writing, but like electronic signitures in the Land Registration Act 2002 something could be done.

A very important part of the real property transaction process is source of funding. The way in which financial intermediaries secure and maintain control over their loan in the absense of a title deed, and an important role for solicitors, is discussed in the section below, Multi-sig accounts.

Multi-sig accounts

Most real properties require some form of funding. This funding is secured by the real property with which it was bought. Currently, until this debt has been paid, the funder kept the title deed. With Ethereum, a multi-sig account can maintain this security.

The multi-sig account would have 3 keys for 3 parties. The owner, the funder, and a solicitor or arbitor.

Hash

"A cryptographic hash function is a hash function which takes an input (or 'message') and returns a fixed-size alphanumeric string, which is called the hash value (sometimes called a message digest, a digital fingerprint, a digest or a checksum)."Wiki

Basic suggestion for implementation

The form that is used to register property, the AP1, an application to change the register, would have another two fields. One for the owner's Bitcoin address and the other for the owner's Ethereum address. Both are an alpha-numberic string of characters. Both are long, and are ideally, and really only, meant for cut and paste due to their length and the importance of what they represent. Bitcoin addresses start with a 1 and Ethereum addresses start with an 0x. This distinction is important, so that they are not mixed up. The current Land Registry database would add two columns to include these two addresses as fields. Each property record would then be updated when a completed AP1 form was submitted.

With one of the Land Registry's Ethereum addresses it would call the relevant contract with the intention to transfer the property to the Ethereum address of the current owner. The Land Registry would basically put the property 'up for sale' on the blockchain, but only the person with access to the Ethereum address that has be registered via the AP1 and in the Land Registry database could 'buy' the property. The owner would send the token Bitcoin or Ethereum payment to the Land Registry and the ownership would be transferred through the transaction. This token amount could be set to cover Land Registry costs.

The code for the smart contract would be need to be written. This is one of the most complex parts of the use of blockchain technology. The contract would contain functions for:

  • first transfer from land registry to registered owner;
  • transfer of ownership;
  • transfer with nominal consideration.

Basically the hash in the smart contract will define ownership. Only the Land Registry can add a hash of the property record to the smart contract by initialising the first 'sale' to the current owner as per their ethereum address on the form AP1. Owners can only change the current hash stored in the smart contract, their ownership of the property. This arrangement guarantees hash validity and means that there can be no other unwanted or unintended additions or changes to the smart contract outside of these two actions.

The Land Registry would initialise the first transfer of ownership to the smart contract. Subsquent transfers would be checked by the Land Registry and any issues concerning transfer would be directed to it.

A hash of the record would be stored and if there were changes to it since the last transaction then this would be flagged and dealt with by the Land Registry.

Multisig is possible to implement in the contract.

The whole title record could be stored on a distributed database like IPFS.

The 32 byte hash stored in the smart contract is the proof of ownership of that property.

The Land Registry:

The blockchain offers a new paradigm, a new model, of how the Land Registry could function.

The technology at the Land Registry is built around the following two things:

The first issue, the storage of records in a database, is addressed by the blockchain. The records currently held by the Land Registry could be transferred onto the blockchain. The overiding reason to transfer records onto the blockchain instead of them being held by the Land Registry is that it greatly improves many aspects of the home buying and selling process.

Basically once a record is on the blockchain that record is immutable, meaning it can't be changed. However, additional information can be added to the record. So a new owner can be added to the record, but the previous owner is still listed on the record, building up a chain of ownership. The last owner of the real property listed on the record is the difinitive one. But not just anyone can add their name to the blockchain. The Land Registry would have control of the first instance of ownership of a property put on the blockchain. The only way for this chain of records to be added to would be through a smart contract.

The blockchain is a chain of blocks. In those blocks are transactions. The blockchain has been called 'The trust protocol'.[2] The basis of the blockchain is that it is peer-to-peer, requiring no third party.

The main reason to transfer the database to the blockchain is a technology built on top of it called Smart Contracts.

Below is a basic introduction to how blockchain technologies relate to, and their use case by, the Land Registry.

Bitcoin is digital cash, but can be seen as the transfer of value between users. Ethereum could also be seen as a transfer of value but it's main function is what's called a smart contract. Both store transactions in a database, but unlike the centralised database of the Land Registry both use a distributed database.

Bitcoin has a database of Unspent Transaction Outputs (UTXO). But its main feature is that it's distributed. A copy of the database is a ledger consisting of blocks in a chain.

One of the main concerns about the blockchain is the removal of intermediaries. This proposal suggests a measured transistion, one record at a time, not a mass migratation. In the fashion that the Land Registry has taken in the past to property registration.

The second issue, the transfer of ownership of the real property, is addressed by the smart contract. The smart contract transfers ownership of the land or property in exchange for consideration.

Bitcoin is used by the buyer to make a payment to the current owner.
